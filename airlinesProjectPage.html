The Challenge

Most who have flown are familiar with in-flight magazines tucked in the seats in front of us. These magazines inspire about various destinations and local lifestyle, but usually don't inform about the destination customers are heading to next. Indeed, it is hard to do so when the same magazine is used for flights between countless destinations. As vacation travel begins to pick up again, travelers will seek to discover more cultural inspiration and crave curated experiences.



As a part of Adobe's Creative Jam with Southwest Airlines, our challenge was to design a third-party tablet app that serves as an editorial experience and travel guide based on a tourists' current location, or future destination; no matter the airline. We wanted to personalize the in-flight entertainment time for the guest using technology in a future-forward way.



How could the in-flight time be used by a passenger to learn about attractions, transport, dining, childcare, and shopping at their destination?


How can the journey be personalized to complement the beginning-to-end vacation experience for a customer?

Interviews


We conducted a series of user interviews with 9 individuals in order to understand what people would want from an editorial-driven in-flight entertainment app. These individuals were travellers in their 20s who have had previous travel experience with friends and family.

Our goal was to find out how these travellers planned their trips - and how an in-flight entertainment app could help them in providing additional information.


Our research told us that users are mostly satisfied with current entertainment options - including movies, shows, and music. However, they use in-flight entertainment as a means to pass-time, rather than viewing content that may enrich their travel experience. Many mentioned that they travel for experiences, and would like there to be entertainment options - editorials in our case - pertaining to their travel experiences.


Our goal should be to transform the in-flight entertainment experience from one of passing time to one of learning and anticipation.

Ideation

After gathering research and defining our goal for this app, we set out to design our app by first defining our target demographic and creating user personas.


Demographic


- Travellers in their 20s travelling with friends or family


We chose to focus primarily on younger travellers because they are more likely to engage with an app and be able to get the most out of it to enhance their travel experience.

Our user persona is Kim, a 20 year old university student who travels in groups with friends or their family. Kim is budget concious and therefore prefers experience-driven activities such as hiking or visiting public attractions, rather than be interested in more expensive activies such as fine-dining. Kim usually plans their trips in groups and does not know much about the destination other than locations they want to visit. They would like to know more about about attractions they can experience and would benefit from information on such experiences.

Features



Cheerful, relaxing visuals


Orange/warm tones to help ease stress drivers encounter and minimize behavioural change by opting to following ride-requesting apps such as Uber and Lyft



Green alternatives


Finding parking may not always be possible during peak hours in busy cities. The app suggests green alternatives to the destination as an option to the user.



Parking timer and notifications


Built in timer that helps users avoid tickets and fines by notifying users about their remaining time. Also allows users to add time if integrated with parking meter.


Results

Designing this mobile application was a great experience. Through creating an interface that allowed minimal time spent on screen, I learned to utilize icons and layouts that are intuitive to navigate and convey information. It is important to note that the actual feasibility of this product would be quite difficult from an engineering perspective - given the lack of technological integration that city parking has. Because of this, it would be difficult to provide comprehensive information to users on the availability of parking as well as to provide the ability to reserve spots. However, through research and collecting information on the current resources available, it is clear that parking is a major obstacle for a large demographic of drivers in metropolitan areas.

With upcoming technologies such as smarthomes and building automation, there is no doubt that one day parking lots will have some form of software integration and management. With this, Parking App can become a feasible product to help find parking.


What I would have done differently?


Use surveys instead of interviews

Because of our one-week time constraint, performing interviews took a lot of our time and in hindsight, it would have been better to perhaps use a survery instead. This would have saved time and reached a larger demographic.


Try to not allow my own travel experiences to affect the solution

As a traveller myself, I found myself reflecting on my own experiences during the design process. Although other travellers had similar experiences to my own with in-flight entertainment apps, it was challenging to stay focused on what our research told us rather than my own experiences.


Take advantage of UI kits

Again due to our time constraints, I wish I had used design systems and assets already available on Adobe XD. This would have saved time when prototyping since we could skip the step of creating individual components. In addition, having pre-made assets helps to create a consistent UI.
